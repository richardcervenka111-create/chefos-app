TICKET 01

Executive summary

Deliverable 1

What Sautero is today: a live, multi-tenant kitchen-management web app (recipes with cost & nutrition, a real-time prep board, ~1,263 priced ingredients, ordering, HACCP labels, working-time tracking, AI assistant) built solo in weeks by a working chef, running on Supabase + GitHub Pages, in genuine daily use by 3 people in one pilot kitchen (Burrito Bandito & Lido, Bern). Zero revenue, zero legal entity, one canton, one founder — who starts a new 80% chef job in roughly two weeks.

Biggest strengths

  • Founder–market fit that money can't buy. A Chef de Partie who has worked London fine dining and Swiss five-star kitchens, building for the exact user he is. Competitors' founders sell to chefs; Richard is one. This is the single most investable fact in the whole project.
  • A live pilot with real data. Not mockups: 173 real recipes, real prep sheets, real supplier price list (Caviezel Giovanettoni) partially imported, real attendance data. Most pre-seed decks would kill for this.
  • Extraordinary build velocity at near-zero burn. ~$45 total AI spend; recipes → prep board → multi-tenant → realtime sync → staging environment in days. Capital efficiency is genuinely elite.
  • Unusual self-honesty in the documentation. Estimated prices are labeled estimated; illustrative ARR is labeled illustrative; the status doc itself says "quality lags scope." Investors reward this; keep it.
  • A disciplined beachhead. Canton Bern first, one named expansion target (Nooch, 3 Bern locations), monetization already reduced to one simple plan.

Biggest weaknesses

  • Capacity math that doesn't close. An 80% kitchen job starting late July + solo development + a public trial on 1 August + CHF 990 onboarding days sold personally. Something in that sentence is false; the plan doesn't say which.
  • Velocity > verification. Dozens of features shipped without ever being opened on a real device; two script-order bugs and one RLS recursion already took the whole app down. The regression checklist exists — it has never been fully run.
  • English UI for a German-speaking market. The Bern brigade — commis, casseroliers — is the actual daily user. This is flagged in the backlog and still unresolved. It is the largest product gap, ahead of any feature.
  • A cost tool built on estimated costs. ~92% of ingredient prices are AI guesses. A chef who spots one wrong price stops trusting every number on the screen.
  • One 547 KB HTML file. Heroic for a prototype; a maintenance wall for a product. No tests, no build, no second contributor possible.
  • No POS integration story. Every serious competitor computes actual vs. theoretical food cost from sales data. Sautero currently can't, and no document mentions it.

Biggest opportunities

  • The chef-led onboarding wedge. "A working chef personally digitizes your kitchen for CHF 990" is a service no VC-funded competitor can credibly offer. It converts Richard's day job from a liability into the sales channel.
  • The line, not the office. MarketMan, Apicbase, MarginEdge live on a manager's laptop weekly. Sautero lives in a cook's wet hands hourly. Daily habitual use is the moat none of them have.
  • DACH German-first gap. Most category leaders are English/Benelux/US-first. A genuinely German-native, Swiss-compliant (nDSG, L-GAV-aware, LEDo allergen declaration) tool has room.
  • Non-dilutive Swiss rails. Venturelab, Innosuisse, cantonal Bern economic development — designed for exactly this profile, no equity cost.
  • Nooch as a repeatable chain play. One branch as a normal customer → recipe-push across 10+ locations is a real 2027 expansion story.

Biggest risks

  • Key-person risk is total. Richard is the developer, the sales team, the support desk, the onboarding service, and the domain expert. One bad month at the new job and Sautero silently stops.
  • Gastro churn. The monetization workshop's own estimate: 3–5%/month — restaurants close. Small ARR erodes fast.
  • Trial failure by logistics, not product. Email login for outside addresses is still broken 19 days before the trial date.
  • Platform dependence. Supabase free tier pauses after 7 days of inactivity; Anthropic API costs are uncapped on the account; GitHub Pages is the production host. All fine — all fragile.
  • Legal exposure ordered exactly backwards. The heaviest-liability features (Working Time = payroll data, Print Labels = food-safety dates) shipped before any lawyer touched anything.
  • Scope gravity. The MVP definition said "prep board + recipes." The product is now seven modules. The pull that caused that is still active.

The market map, honestly drawn

How Sautero becomes the category leader (of the right category)

1 · Own the line, refuse the office

Every incumbent's daily active user is a manager. Sautero's is a cook. Win the surface that gets touched 40 times a shift — prep board, labels, "what's 86'd" — and the back-office modules become your upsell later, instead of you being their afterthought. Never chase inventory/accounting head-on; you lose on features and funding.

2 · Sell the chef, not the software

The onboarding service (a working chef digitizes your kitchen, CHF 990) is the one move no competitor can copy — their founders can't hold a knife. Every onboarding produces a reference kitchen, verified local prices, and a founding subscriber. The service is the go-to-market.

3 · Be German-native and Swiss-legal

German-first UI, CHF-first, LEDo allergen declarations, nDSG-clean data handling, L-GAV-aware working time. The US giants won't bother; the DACH incumbents are office-shaped. "Built in a Bern kitchen, in your language, for your Lebensmittelkontrolle" is a positioning nobody else can say.

4 · Turn prep data into the moat

Nobody on the map has line-level execution data: real prep times, real par levels, real 86 patterns per station per day. Two years of that in 50 kitchens powers forecasting nobody can replicate — if the data promises in legal.html are honored on the way (opt-in, real anonymity, 2028 not 2026).

TICKET 06

UI recommendations

Deliverable 5 · short, because the system already exists

Sautero already has what most pre-seed products lack: a coherent identity (navy/teal kitchen-notebook, unified across app and docs as of 07-13). The job now is codify and stop, not redesign. Enterprise-SaaS best practice at this stage is a frozen system, ruthlessly reused.

Codify the tokens

One CSS custom-property block is the design system: 6 colors, 3 type roles, 2 radii, 3 spacing steps. Everything in the app inherits; nothing gets a bespoke style. This also makes an eventual multi-file refactor mechanical instead of archaeological.

Status colors are semantics, not decoration

TO DO / CHECK / FINISH / DON'T DO and 🚩 low-stock must be distinguishable at 2 metres, at an angle, through glare — and never rely on hue alone (deuteranopia is ~5% of male cooks): pair color with shape/weight.

Empty states direct action

Every empty screen answers "what do I do?": empty Check List → "Scan your paper prep sheet"; empty Ingredients → "Import your supplier price list". Emptiness is your onboarding funnel, per the writing rule: an empty screen is an invitation to act.

Typography: two sizes fewer

Dense data screens (Ingredients, Check List) have accumulated micro-variants. Collapse to a strict scale (12/14/16/20/28). Consistency reads as reliability — which is the brand promise of a HACCP tool.

Motion budget: zero new

The sticky-header collapse and undo toasts are enough. Every added animation is battery, jank on old Androids in the device matrix you haven't tested yet, and review surface.

German copy is a design task

When DE localization lands, the register matters: kitchen German ("mise", "86", "auf Stellung") not office German. Have the pilot brigade sanity-check every Check List string — wrong register reads as tourist software.

TICKET 10

Exit strategy — the honest version

Deliverable 9 · replacing the fantasy buyer list with the real one

Direct, because you asked for it: today Sautero has no acquisition value. Acquirers buy revenue, retention data, customer relationships, or a team — Sautero has none yet in transactable form. The code itself is worth roughly its rebuild cost, which AI has made small. That's not an insult; it's the starting line of every company. The question is what creates value from here, and who realistically pays for it.

Who realistically buys a Swiss kitchen-ops SaaS

  • DACH vertical consolidators — Apicbase, FoodNotify, gastronovi, Choco, or PE-backed roll-ups of hospitality software. They buy market entry: a Swiss customer base + German-Swiss product. The most probable acquirer class by far.
  • Swiss POS/payments players — a Lightspeed-type wanting a back-of-house story for its Swiss base; integration partner today, acquirer later.
  • Food-service suppliers — Transgourmet (Coop), Pistor, Saviva: a tool inside their customers' kitchens that shapes order flow is strategically priceless to them at even modest scale. The most interesting strategic buyer if the Order List → supplier path materializes.
  • An operator group — a Swiss gastro group acquihiring the tool + the chef-founder for internal use. The modest but real floor outcome.

Who does not buy this — ever, at this shape

Oracle, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, OpenAI, Salesforce, SAP, Block, DoorDash, Uber, Sysco, US Foods, Toast, PAR, NCR, Fiserv. These acquire at tens of millions in ARR, in their home markets, with teams. Putting them on a pre-seed slide costs credibility with any investor who has seen one before. The earlier strategic guidance on this stands: smaller DACH vertical players and Swiss non-dilutive funding are the real path.

Comparable-transaction reality: small vertical SaaS in Europe trades at roughly 3–6× ARR unless a strategic buyer needs the asset (data, geography, supplier flow), where premiums happen. Everything below uses that band.

TICKET 13

Investor readiness — would I invest?

Deliverable 12 · written as the investor across the table

As a pre-seed VC: no — not yet, and honestly, maybe never needed

Why not: solo part-time founder (the #1 pass reason in Europe, before anything else is read), zero revenue, 3 users, no entity, and a market framing — one Swiss canton — that can't return a fund. No priced round is justifiable today at any sensible valuation; raising now would mean bad terms for a story that gets strictly better with 90 days of patience.

What would flip it: 5–10 paying kitchens with week-8 retention · churn data · German UI · a capacity answer (co-founder, contractor, or a credible solo-with-services plan) · and a founder who has chosen venture deliberately rather than by default. At that point a CHF 1.5–3M-cap SAFE conversation becomes normal Swiss pre-seed territory.

As the honest advisor: the better question is which game?

Sautero's economics — near-zero cost base, services cash flow, tiny beachhead — are a textbook bootstrap, and an awkward venture story. Venture demands DACH-or-bigger ambition, a team, and dilution; bootstrapping demands only what already exists plus discipline. Both are legitimate. Drifting between them is not: it produces founding prices set like a bootstrapper, decks written like a VC case, and a founder answering to neither logic.

Recommendation: run the trial, take the founding revenue, apply for Innosuisse/Venturelab (non-dilutive keeps both doors open), and make the venture-vs-bootstrap decision explicitly in Q1 2027 with real retention data on the table. Until then, no investor meetings — only investor-ready documents.

What's missing entirely

Deliverable 15 · absent from all nineteen documents

Business-critical gaps

  • POS / sales data. Not one document mentions it — yet it's the ceiling on the food-cost value proposition and the thing every serious competitor has.
  • Insurance. Berufshaftpflicht is named once (monetization) with no owner or date. Labels + payroll data without it is uninsured liability.
  • A support model. What happens when a paying kitchen breaks at 18:30 Saturday and the founder is on a hot line until 23:00? Even an honest written SLA ("next morning") beats silence.
  • Churn & cancellation flow. No offboarding, no data-export UX, no exit survey — the founding offer promises exports "on request" with no mechanism.
  • Accounting reality. First invoice implies bookkeeping, AHV contributions, tax handling of prepaid annual revenue. Zero pages on it.

Product & technical gaps

  • Any automated test. The regression list is manual; the bug class it targets (script order) is exactly what a 5-line smoke test in CI catches for free.
  • Restore-tested backups. Flagged in the backlog, still open — the single scariest line in the stack given real pilot data is in production.
  • Performance budget. A 547 KB HTML on a 2019 Android over kitchen WiFi — never measured.
  • Multi-tenant ingredient split. The known architecture conflict (shared vs. per-kitchen ingredient table) is logged as an open decision and blocks clean scaling of pricing.
  • Recipe IP provenance. The workshop's lawyer flagged it: some of the 173 recipes date from previous employers. Quietly audit before they're a paid product's content.

Founder-system gaps

  • A sustainability plan. 80% kitchen job + solo SaaS has a well-known failure mode and no document addresses hours, rest, or what gets dropped in a bad week. This is the real risk register.
  • A bus-factor note. If Richard is offline for a month, can anyone even access Supabase? A sealed-envelope credentials plan is one evening's work.
  • Competitor watch. Cucinovo-class entrants appeared between two of your own documents. A quarterly 30-minute scan, calendared.
  • A definition of "enough." Bootstrap vs. venture (Ticket 13) is ultimately this question. Writing it down once will settle a hundred future micro-decisions.
